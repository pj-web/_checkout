On perseverance and change
Two frogs fell into a farmer's bucket. Frog 1, Barrius, was a pond-half-empty sort of amphibian and quickly concluded all was lost, quickly drowning. Frog 2, Augustine, wouldn’t give up however – he kept swimming and swimming and swimming and wouldn’t stop. Milk would’ve curdled into butter from the amount he kicked his little froggie legs and he would’ve been able to hop out. Unfortunately, this particular bucket was filled with water and so, in the end, Augustine also drowned. Point is, that – sometimes and despite all the best will in the world – things move against us. However, change is constant; what’s bleak now may become beautiful in the future. All we can do is persevere and hope, because that next bucket may be filled with milk after all. Yuga Labs goes ape on Ryder Ripps
The Bored Ape Yacht Club has had some very serious allegations levied at it recently, but its founders are swinging into action to defend the project. Talk about guerilla warfare.
“We’ve become the target of a crazy disinformation campaign” wrote Yuga Labs’ Gordon Goner (Wylie Aronow, IRL), after a video went viral examining accusations that BAYC contains hidden Nazi and racist messages, plus promoting a knock off NFT set as a form of consumer protest.
The founders are now suing their accuser, the artist Ryder Ripps, for trying to devalue BAYC NFTs and “scamming consumers”. Meanwhile, others have been examining Ripps’ claims in more detail: a recent deep dive into co-founder Greg Solano’s online history didn’t find any evidence to support the accusations against him.
How is APE coin doing with all this drama, you ask? Well, not badly actually. The token is on its fifth day of gains after a 13% gain last week, and rallied 16% on Sunday before settling down somewhat after a new Bored Ape-themed video with Eminem and Snoop Dogg garnered some excitement.

A big week for Smith & Wesson
Shares of firearms manufacturer Smith & Wesson Brands are rising after a solid earnings report comes in, hot on the heels of a piece of legislative news.
The stock gained nearly 23% last week to enjoy its best week in almost exactly a year. Friday’s 14% rally marked its best day of 2022 and came after the gunmaker announced better than expected earnings-per-share at $0.82, as well as revenue that‘s fallen 44% but still beat analyst estimates.
The Supreme Court was responsible for the rest of the gains. Last Thursday, the country’s highest court established the right to carry firearms in New York city, a v controversial decision that has favorable implications for the gun industry. It’ll be a while before the impact of the decision is reflected on the balance sheet, but next quarter’s earnings will reveal more.
Gunmakers’ stocks were on the rise across the board last week, especially after the horrific Uvalde shooting got people anticipating stricter gun laws. Stocks tied to weapons and ammunition supply are usually bought by individuals because not a lot of indexes or ETFs hold them, and many institutions are restricted from buying them.

Solana causes a stir
Hold the phone, cause Solana’s on a relief rally after a new development from its team creates chatter in the cryptoverse.
Prices soared over 9% on Friday to build on their 11% gains on Thursday, with the token enjoying its best week since the end of March – though it’s far from recovered from the 10 weeks of misery it has had since then. The surge came on the back of a new mobile platform release on Thursday, but that’s not all…
Solana has unveiled its very own crypto smartphone, which is dubbed the Solana Saga. The announcement elicited a mixed range of reactions from the crypto community, with some even calling the company the “Apple of Web3” and saying that Solana is becoming an even more formidable rival to Ethereum.
But others couldn’t help voice their concern that Solana needs to get its house in order before taking on something like this. The network has had over six outages this year, contributing to recent price declines, and Cardano co-founder Charles Hoskinson even poked fun at the new feature and implied it would suffer similar interruptions. Copper Canyon
The papers may be filled with oil and energy updates atm, but elsewhere the precious metals market is struggling through multi-year lows.
Industrial metals are going through their worst crash since the 2008 financial crisis as prices feel the impact of growing recession worries. It’s a dramatic turnaround from the last two years, which saw metals prices soar thanks to post-covid bullishness and supply snarls.
Copper has been one of the hardest hit, tumbling into a bear market after finding a new all-time high only a few months ago. The metal is often considered an economic bellwether and is down over 20% in the last three months to sit at its lowest prices since Feb 2021, though the CEO of copper giant Codelco sees a “very firm” price ahead for the commodity despite the downturn.
Global manufacturing growth is on the slowdown, and other industrial metals like nickel and tin had one of their worst weeks since 2005 last week. On the bright side, some investors think that slowing growth and slumping commodities prices may reduce pressure on the economy and the need for hawkish rate hikes.

Carnival cruises through earnings
Carnival gives us a little peek into how the cruise industry is surviving economic headwinds, and it looks like smooth sailing ahead for now matey.
Shares of Carnival sailed ahead by 13% on Friday after hitting its lowest levels since April 2020 (when covid hit the market) the week before. Its Q2 earnings report was to thank, and was buoying up other cruise stocks as well, with Royal Caribbean and Norwegian Cruise Lines both rallying nearly 16%.
Revenue, booking volumes, and occupancy are all on the rise. Even though revenue growth of nearly 50% still missed estimates, revenue per passenger cruise day is only slightly below pre-pandemic levels, and its cruises are now operating at 91% capacity with plans to hit 110% capacity in the coming months.
Have cruise stocks outsailed covid? While the company reinforced the continued strength in demand recently, with near-term bookings outpacing 2019 levels, it also said that covid, inflation, and higher fuel prices were defos having a material impact on the business and would lead to a net loss for the current quarter and full year.
